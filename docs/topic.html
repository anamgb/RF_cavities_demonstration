1 RF cavities in conventional accelerators

Conventional accelerators use metallic chambers that contain a localised, oscillating radio-frequency (RF) electromagnetic field. These accelerating structures are called radio-frequency (RF) cavities. When charged particles (electrons, ions, etc.) are injected into the cavity with correct timing with the field, they experience an accelerating electric field along the beam axis and gain energy.

The cavity is shaped so that it resonates: electromagnetic waves build up in intensity inside it, producing a much stronger accelerating field than a single pass of the wave would give. This increases the energy a particle gains per metre of cavity, known as accelerating gradient.

2 The LC-circuit analogy

An RF cavity behaves in many ways like an LC resonator circuit. In an LC circuit, the capacitor stores energy in its electric field and the inductor stores energy in its magnetic field. The capacitor (capacitance \(C\)) first charges fully, then discharges through the inductor (inductance \(L\)); the magnetic field in the inductor peaks and, as it collapses, it recharges the capacitor. Energy travels back and forth between electric and magnetic form, oscillating at the resonant frequency \(f_0\):

\[f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{L \cdot C}}\]

In an RF cavity the same energy exchange happens, but between the electromagnetic field’s electric and magnetic components rather than between two separate components: the oscillating electric field generates a changing magnetic field, which in turn regenerates the electric field. For an accelerating mode, the electric field points along the beam axis. If an electron (or another charged particle) bunch arrives in phase with the field, it is pushed forward on every crossing.

3 Why a metal box acts like an RF cavity

When an RF signal is fed through a probe into a hollow metallic box with conducting walls, electromagnetic waves travel back and forth and reflect off the walls. If a reflected wave returns in phase with the incoming wave, the two interfere constructively and the field grows stronger with every reflection. Only wavelengths that “fit” the box’s dimensions satisfy this condition and form standing waves, so the box behaves as a closed electromagnetic resonator with a set of discrete resonant frequencies (its modes).

4 Quality factor (Q-factor)

The quality factor tells you how efficiently a cavity stores energy once an RF signal has been sent in. Think of a bell: one that rings for a long time after being struck has a high Q; one whose sound dies away quickly has a low Q.

Q is measured, for example, from the sharpness of the resonance peak:

\[Q = \frac{f_0}{\Delta f}\]

Where \(\Delta f\) is the \(−3 dB\) (half-power) bandwidth (the width of the peak between the two points where the response has dropped to 0.707 of its maximum).

A tall, narrow peak means a high \(Q\). Changing the cavity’s shape changes which wavelengths it can hold, and therefore shifts its resonant frequency.

5 Effect of adding a dielectric material

The resonant frequency depends on both the geometry and the speed of wave propagation inside. In an air or vacuum filled cavity, waves travel at essentially the speed of light, \(c\).

Introducing a dielectric raises the relative permittivity \(\varepsilon_r\), which slows the waves down. For a cavity completely filled with a dielectric:

\[f = \frac{f_0}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_r}}\] A partial filling gives an intermediate shift that depends on where the dielectric is relative to the field.
